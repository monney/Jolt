Jolt

An intelligent alarm system for students

Overview

Our WatchOS app Jolt will use the Apple Watch’s heart rate monitor and accelerometer to determine if a student has inadvertently fallen asleep and then vibrate and play an alarm if they have.

Elevator Pitch

Jolt is an intelligent alarm system to help students get through work when they’re tired. Current solutions include apps that try to wake up drivers who are falling asleep at the wheel: such a situation has an extremely low error tolerance, and the Apple Watch is unable to detect sleep that quickly.

Jolt monitors a user's heartrate and acceleration, and uses a machine learning algorithm to detect the onset of sleep. If sleep is detected, the person will be woken up via alarm/vibration. The functionality we are going for has a high enough error tolerance that the Apple Watch’s capabilities are sufficiently powerful Moreover, the customization we provide allows for flexibility that covers a variety of common use cases. In the future, we will be implement features such as alarms for calendar events, or even using the Watch to automatically text a friend for particularly heavy sleepers who sleep past their alarms.

Ultimately, we recognize that working when you're tired feels pretty awful, and we decided to create Jolt to make that process a little less terrible for everyone.

Requirements and Target Audiences

Target Audience

The target audience will be college students who need to stay awake while studying or for an important event.

Key User Benefits

The ability to...

  • Wake up shortly after inadvertently falling asleep

  • Schedule an interval of allowable sleep time

  • Contact friends to wake you up in the case of important events

System Improvements

The current systems are geared towards drivers who are falling asleep at the wheel. The primary flaw with this is that to be useful in this situation you need to detect sleep within milliseconds, which is infeasible by most methods. With non life-threatening situations the tolerance for error is much higher. Other iOS apps to track sleep are designed for all night usage and monitoring sleep quality, but with most smart watches having a single day’s battery life, you lose the critical function of charging while sleeping.

Functionality

Common Use Cases

  • Our student “Joe” has finals and hasn’t been able to get much sleep over the past week, but he really needs to study.

    Joe activates the app and sets it to monitor his activity while he is studying. If he accidentally falls asleep while he is studying, the app will detect this and wake him up.

  • Joe needs to sleep and can’t study anymore, he doesn’t think he can fall asleep right now. But, if he does, he wants to sleep for thirty minutes

    Joe sets the app to monitor his activity, once we detect that Joe has been sleeping for thirty minutes, we will wake him up.

  • Joe is in lecture but it’s at 8am and he stayed up late studying

    Joe activates the app while he is in lecture and the watch will silently vibrate and wake Joe up if he falls asleep.

  • Joe is a heavy sleeper and he needs to be up for a midterm

    Joe adds the time of his exam to our app, we will detect if he is sleeping before he needs to be at his exam and attempt to wake him up with an alarm if he is. If we are unable to, we will send a message to a designated friend informing them of this.

Design

Front-End

The front-end will be designed in Photoshop and built in Xcode with AutoLayout.

Front-End Back-End Connection

The front-end will connect to the back-end through segue’s and notification centers.

Back-End

The back-end will be created in Swift and use WatchKit and HealthKit.

Back-End Database Connection

The back-end database is the collection of images we are using for animations and the HealthKit data in the HealthKit Store and Core Motion data.

Database

The database will be implemented using Core Data and SQLite. The backend will store data used to help with making accurate sleep predictions.

Testing

Testing will consist of unit testing using Xcode, functional testing by manually testing on the Apple Watch, and user testing by having potential users test the app.

Miscellaneous

The tools chosen are standard for iOS wearables development.

Risks and Outcomes

  • App store approval takes a while so it likely won’t be launched by the end of the semester. This is not too big of a risk as we will be able to launch before next school year.

  • Not many people currently have smart watches so the scope for the app will be limited initially. This will be solved as the appeal of smart watches grows and Apple launches the second version of the Apple Watch this fall (3 million additional units on college campuses alone).

  • User testing will be difficult as not many people on campus have extensive experience with Smart Watch interfaces. This risk will be minimized by rigorously following Apple’s human interaction guidelines for wearable devices.
